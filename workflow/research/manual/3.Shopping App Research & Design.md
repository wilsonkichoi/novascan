# **Architectural Research and Discovery: Analytics, Chatbot, and UI Integration for Serverless Receipt Processing Platform**

## **Phase 1: Business Objectives, High-Level Design Intent, and Constraints**

The architectural foundation of any modern enterprise application relies heavily on the rigor applied during the initial Research and Discovery phase. In adherence to the established Phase 1 workflow standard, this period of investigation requires a meticulous alignment between overarching business imperatives, the high-level design intent of the digital product, and the immutable technical constraints imposed by the deployment environment.1 For the development of a next-generation shopping receipts scanner application spanning both web and mobile platforms, the primary business objective is to translate raw, commoditized transactional data into highly actionable, personalized financial intelligence.4 By providing end-users with immediate, intuitive visibility into their spending behaviors, the platform aims to foster financial literacy, drive sustained daily active user engagement, and establish a competitive moat within the personal finance software market.5 Consequently, this engaged user base generates high-value, anonymized analytical datasets that can be leveraged for broader market insights, peer-group benchmarking, and downstream monetization strategies.4

The high-level design intent of this platform centers on establishing a frictionless, highly responsive user experience seamlessly coupled with a zero-maintenance, highly scalable backend.3 Modern user expectations dictate that mobile dashboards must render in single-digit milliseconds, and conversational AI interfaces must maintain natural, human-like pacing, thereby heavily restricting the acceptable complexity and latency of synchronous backend processing.8 Furthermore, the user interface must be rapidly iterative, capable of reflecting continuous design updates without incurring the massive engineering overhead typically associated with native mobile development.11 Consequently, the frontend architecture is mandated to rely on emerging AI-driven design-to-code pipelines, while the backend relies entirely on an event-driven, microservices-oriented serverless paradigm deployed on Amazon Web Services (AWS).10 This intent dictates that all compute, storage, and database layers must scale automatically and proportionally in response to user traffic, ensuring that the system remains highly available during peak retail periods while scaling to absolute zero during idle periods.14

However, this ambitious design intent is strictly bounded by specific architectural and financial constraints defined during the discovery process.1 The foremost constraint is the mandate for extreme cost efficiency, particularly concerning standing compute capacity and idle infrastructure. In traditional, server-based architectures, always-on vector databases and persistent container orchestration platforms consume substantial capital regardless of active user concurrency.14 The explicit serverless constraint necessitates the total elimination of these fixed operational costs.15 This requires the careful, deliberate selection of database technologies and compute primitives that charge exclusively per execution duration, per request, or per read/write capacity unit.17 Additionally, the architecture is constrained by the assigned scope boundaries: while the system relies heavily on data derived from scanned receipts, the internal mechanics of the receipt ingestion pipeline and the underlying Optical Character Recognition (OCR) processing via AWS Textract fall outside the purview of this specific architectural phase and are treated as external, upstream dependencies providing structured JSON payloads to the analytical layer.

## **Frontend Integration Strategy: Google Stitch to AWS Backend**

The transition from visual design mockups to production-ready frontend code has historically represented a significant bottleneck in the software development lifecycle, characterized by high organizational friction, communication breakdowns, and manual translation errors.19 To circumvent these systemic inefficiencies, the architectural strategy for the frontend integration leverages Google Stitch, an AI-native UI design canvas that fundamentally collapses the traditional design-to-development workflow.11

### **The Paradigm Shift of Google Stitch and Vibe Design**

Google Stitch, powered by the Gemini 2.5 Pro multimodal artificial intelligence engine, operates on an emerging philosophy termed "vibe design".12 This paradigm allows product managers, designers, and engineers to generate high-fidelity, interactive user interfaces by providing natural language descriptions of business objectives, uploading reference images, or supplying rough hand-drawn sketches.11 Unlike legacy tools that require manual manipulation of vectors and layers, Stitch synthesizes these unstructured inputs into precise layout primitives, semantic hierarchical structures, and theme-aware components.12

It is vital to delineate the operational boundaries between Google Stitch and traditional design software such as Figma. Stitch is optimized for the rapid exploration phase of product development, eliminating the "first draft friction" that typically stalls iterative velocity.19 Its infinite canvas and voice-enabled conversational editing capabilities allow creators to explore multiple layout variations instantaneously, assessing aesthetic directions and structural paradigms without committing hours to manual pixel-pushing.21 However, Stitch is not entirely intended to replace the rigorous design system management, complex team collaboration, and pixel-level refinement that Figma provides.19 Instead, the architecture supports a bifurcated workflow: Stitch generates the robust initial layouts and conceptual prototypes, which can then be optionally exported to Figma for granular downstream refinement, or, more crucially for this highly automated project, exported directly into the development pipeline via specific integration protocols.19

### **The DESIGN.md Specification and Model Context Protocol (MCP)**

The most critical technical integration mechanism provided by Google Stitch is the DESIGN.md specification.11 Unlike static image exports, disjointed CSS snippets, or proprietary binary design files, DESIGN.md serves as a highly portable, agent-readable markdown document that explicitly encodes the entirety of the application's design system.11 This file standardizes typography scales, color palettes, spacing tokens, and component interaction behaviors into a centralized source of truth.11 The introduction of DESIGN.md significantly improves cross-platform consistency, as any modification to the core system automatically cascades to all linked UI elements within the generative model's context.25

To bridge the substantial gap between this abstract design specification and executable application code, the architecture employs the Model Context Protocol (MCP).20 The MCP acts as a secure, standardized communication layer connecting disparate AI tools. Using a dedicated MCP server—such as the Cubis Foundry MCP or the Antigravity IDE integration—autonomous coding agents (like Claude Code or Cursor) can directly ingest the DESIGN.md file alongside the visual UI artifacts generated by Stitch.20

The workflow is highly automated. The coding agent reads the natural language business rules, analyzes the structural layouts generated by Stitch, parses the rigid constraints defined within the DESIGN.md file, and autonomously outputs strictly typed frontend code.27 This process maps abstract visual concepts into concrete implementation briefs, translating UI artifacts into framework-specific routing, state management, and styling primitives without relying on human interpretation.27 This effectively eliminates the traditional designer-to-developer handoff phase, creating a unified feedback loop where a single, dynamic context file drives both visual design iteration and the subsequent code generation, drastically accelerating the time-to-market for new features.20

### **Mobile Framework Recommendation: Flutter**

Given the project requirement to deploy the shopping receipts application seamlessly across both web and mobile environments, the architectural recommendation for the client-side framework is Flutter.29 Created by Google, Flutter is an open-source, declarative UI framework that enables developers to build natively compiled applications for mobile, web, and desktop architectures from a single, unified codebase.29 This inherently reduces the engineering maintenance burden by half compared to maintaining distinct iOS (Swift) and Android (Kotlin) repositories.

Flutter utilizes the Dart programming language, which was explicitly designed to support highly reactive frameworks and fluid visual behaviors.29 Its widget-centric paradigm aligns perfectly with the structured component outputs generated by AI coding agents reading from the DESIGN.md specification. When an MCP-enabled agent interprets the design tokens from Google Stitch, it can systematically map these styles directly into Flutter's global ThemeData classes. This ensures that all generated UI components—from complex spending charts to infinite-scrolling transaction lists—automatically inherit the correct branding, typography, and spatial rules without manual styling interventions.27 Furthermore, Flutter is highly compatible with next-generation generative SDKs, such as the GenUI SDK, which allows for the dynamic, runtime generation of UI widgets based on conversational contexts—an essential capability for the application's planned interactive AI chatbot.31

### **AWS Backend API Integration Patterns**

The integration between the Flutter frontend and the AWS serverless backend is orchestrated primarily via AWS Amplify.30 AWS Amplify provides a comprehensive, declarative toolkit and hosting platform for connecting cross-platform mobile applications to robust cloud resources.30 It abstracts the complexity of infrastructure provisioning, offering streamlined implementations for secure user authentication, offline data synchronization, real-time event subscriptions, and continuous integration/continuous deployment (CI/CD) pipelines.30

The recommended integration pattern avoids exposing direct database endpoints to the public internet. Instead, the architecture utilizes a GraphQL API layer powered by AWS AppSync, which is fully managed through the Amplify configuration.32 GraphQL is distinctly superior to traditional REST APIs in mobile contexts because it allows the Flutter client to define the precise shape and scope of the data it requires in a single network request. If the spending dashboard only requires the top three categories and the total variance percentage, the GraphQL query retrieves exactly those fields, minimizing payload size, reducing parsing overhead, and preserving mobile battery life and radio bandwidth.32

The end-to-end integration workflow operates as follows. First, the Flutter client securely authenticates the user via Amazon Cognito, obtaining a time-limited JSON Web Token (JWT).32 The user interface, initially generated via the Stitch-to-Flutter MCP pipeline, dispatches an Amplify GraphQL query requesting specific analytical data.32 AWS AppSync receives this authenticated query, validates the permissions, and routes the request to highly specific AWS Lambda resolvers. These serverless Lambda functions execute the necessary business logic, interface with the underlying analytical databases to retrieve the aggregated financial metrics, and format the response back through AppSync to the Flutter client.32 This decoupled, event-driven architecture ensures that the mobile UI remains wholly ignorant of the backend database mechanics, allowing the data layer to evolve independently without requiring simultaneous mobile application updates.

## **Analytical Data Model and User Spending Dashboard**

A cornerstone feature of the receipt scanner application is the interactive user spending dashboard. This interface must provide users with instantaneous, accurate insights into their financial behaviors, highlighting top spending categories, plotting historical trends, and contrasting individual spending against heavily anonymized, aggregated peer-group averages. Designing the underlying data layer for this dashboard requires navigating a complex architectural tradeoff: balancing the high-throughput, write-heavy ingestion of incoming receipt data against the ultra-low-latency read requirements of the mobile user interface.9

### **Database Selection: The Dichotomy of Timestream and DynamoDB**

The analytical requirements of the application present a fundamental dichotomy in database selection, highlighting the distinct differences between Online Transaction Processing (OLTP) and Online Analytical Processing (OLAP) systems in a serverless environment. Raw receipt transactions are inherently time-series data. They consist of continuous, immutable measurements (the monetary value of the transaction) inextricably bound to specific chronological timestamps and descriptive dimensions (such as merchant identifiers, categorization tags, and geographical locations).9

For the foundational storage and deep analysis of this data, Amazon Timestream emerges as the optimal technology.9 Timestream is a fully managed, serverless, purpose-built time-series database capable of ingesting trillions of events and executing complex temporal analytics at a speed mathematically faster than traditional relational databases, and at a significantly reduced fraction of the cost.9 It excels at queries that must scan massive historical datasets, group by multiple dimensions, and utilize built-in temporal interpolation and smoothing functions.9 Timestream handles data tiering automatically, writing recent data to a high-performance memory store and gracefully transitioning older records to a cost-effective magnetic store for long-term retention.9

However, while Timestream is highly efficient for broad analytical queries encompassing thousands of records, its response times for complex aggregations often exceed one full second.36 This latency profile is fundamentally incompatible with the single-digit millisecond response times required for snappy, responsive mobile dashboard rendering.9 Conversely, Amazon DynamoDB, a serverless NoSQL key-value store, guarantees single-digit millisecond performance at virtually any scale, making it the premier choice for serving data directly to a frontend application.9 Yet, DynamoDB is structurally and economically poorly suited for computing ad-hoc mathematical aggregations over millions of rows, as this requires expensive full table scans, consumes massive read-capacity units, and encounters strict pagination and item size limits.9 In specific use cases involving frequent metric updates, the cost differential is staggering; specific query patterns in Timestream costing $1.65 per day can be executed via precomputed DynamoDB reads for fractions of a penny ($0.000068 per day).38

The optimal architectural pattern, therefore, is a hybrid approach that leverages the strengths of both systems.36 Amazon Timestream acts as the system of record and primary analytical data store, retaining the raw, immutable time-series data derived upstream from the receipt processing pipeline. To satisfy the low-latency requirements of the frontend, asynchronous background processes (AWS Lambda functions triggered by Amazon EventBridge schedules or direct data insertion events) periodically precompute the specific metrics required by the mobile dashboard.36 These Lambda functions execute the heavy analytical queries against Timestream, format the aggregated results, and write these pre-calculated values into DynamoDB.36 The Flutter mobile application then queries DynamoDB exclusively for its display data, achieving near-instantaneous load times while completely shielding the expensive analytical engine from the volatile, high-volume read traffic generated by the user base.36

| Database Service | Architectural Role | Operational Strengths | Structural Limitations |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Amazon Timestream** | System of Record, Deep Analytics | Optimized for immutable time-series data; built-in temporal interpolation; infinite scale without manual sharding.9 | Slower point queries (frequently \>1 second); lacks transactional in-place updates; complex backfilling rules.36 |
| **Amazon DynamoDB** | Dashboard Serving Layer | Guaranteed single-digit millisecond latency; highly flexible key-value lookups; extremely cost-effective for targeted, precomputed reads.9 | Prohibitively expensive for massive ad-hoc aggregations or full table scans; strict 400KB size limit per item.36 |
| **Amazon Athena** | Batch Peer-Group Analytics | Serverless distributed SQL across S3 data lakes; highly effective for massive, system-wide aggregations.4 | High latency profile; entirely unsuitable for real-time mobile application interaction.4 |

### **Dashboard Data Schema and Query Structure**

The analytical data model within Amazon Timestream must be meticulously structured to maximize query efficiency. Each individual receipt entry is inserted as a distinct record. The MeasureName is defined as transaction\_amount, and the MeasureValue represents the monetary cost of the purchase. The contextual metadata—such as user\_id, category\_id, merchant\_name, and location\_zip—are stored as Dimensions.36 This append-only structure prevents update conflicts and allows SQL queries to group, filter, and aggregate seamlessly across the temporal axis.36

To populate the user spending dashboard, an AWS Lambda function executes a specifically optimized query against Timestream to aggregate the top spending categories for the current month. The SQL structure is designed to isolate the user's data and sum the measures rapidly:

SQL

SELECT category\_id, SUM(measure\_value::double) as total\_spent  
FROM "ReceiptAnalytics"."Transactions"  
WHERE time between ago(30d) and now()  
AND user\_id \= 'user\_123'  
GROUP BY category\_id  
ORDER BY total\_spent DESC  
LIMIT 5

Upon receiving the result set, the Lambda function updates a specific, pre-allocated record in DynamoDB. The DynamoDB table is designed utilizing advanced single-table design principles. The Partition Key (PK) is defined as USER\#\<user\_id\>, ensuring all data related to a specific user resides on the same physical storage partition for optimized retrieval. The Sort Key (SK) is defined as DASHBOARD\#CURRENT\_MONTH.9 The attributes of this item contain a compressed JSON payload representing the precomputed categories and total values. When the Flutter app requests the dashboard view, AppSync performs a highly targeted GetItem operation on this precise key combination, bypassing computational overhead entirely and returning the data in milliseconds.

### **Peer-Group Averages Calculation Pattern**

Calculating comparison averages (e.g., comparing a user's grocery spending against the median spending of other users in their specific geographical state) introduces a substantially heavier computational burden. This operation requires scanning and analyzing data across the entire platform's user base rather than isolating a single user's records.4 Attempting to perform this massive aggregation in real-time as a synchronous response to a mobile application request is a severe architectural anti-pattern that guarantees timeout failures and skyrocketing compute costs.13

Instead, the system utilizes a decoupled, asynchronous batch processing pipeline. Raw transactional data is periodically exported or streamed into an Amazon S3 data lake. For large-scale peer aggregations, the architecture leverages Amazon Athena, an interactive query service that facilitates the analysis of data directly in Amazon S3 using standard SQL.4 Athena performs distributed, map-reduce style queries across the entire data lake to calculate average spending thresholds per category and demographic segment, ensuring that all data utilized for peer comparisons is thoroughly anonymized to protect user privacy.

A scheduled AWS Step Functions state machine orchestrates this complex workflow on a nightly or weekly cadence. Once Athena completes the aggregation, the resulting peer-group benchmarks are bulk-loaded into DynamoDB under generic, highly accessible keys (e.g., PK: PEER\_GROUP\#ZIP\_90210, SK: CATEGORY\#GROCERIES). During standard application operation, the mobile backend (via AppSync and Lambda) retrieves the user's personal spending total and concurrently fetches the relevant, precomputed peer-group average from DynamoDB. The backend calculates the percentage variance in memory and delivers the contextual comparison to the Flutter frontend, maintaining strict latency budgets while providing deep analytical insights.

## **AI Chatbot Architecture and Context Management**

Beyond static visual dashboards, modern users expect highly interactive, conversational interfaces. The application incorporates an advanced AI chatbot that enables users to interrogate their complex financial data using natural language, supporting both text-based queries and seamless voice interfaces. Delivering this functionality requires a robust, context-aware conversational architecture powered by advanced foundation models.39

### **The Amazon Nova Foundation Model Family**

The core cognitive intelligence of the chatbot is driven entirely by the Amazon Nova model family, a suite of foundation models designed to deliver frontier-level reasoning capabilities coupled with industry-leading price-performance ratios.41 Given the strict constraints on operational costs and the absolute necessity for rapid response times in a consumer-facing mobile application, the architecture specifically utilizes Amazon Nova 2 Lite for text-based interactions and complex reasoning tasks.8

Nova 2 Lite provides exceptional intelligence for everyday workloads, matching or explicitly exceeding the benchmark capabilities of peer models while operating at highly disruptive cost levels (approximately $0.06 per million input tokens and $0.24 per million output tokens).8 Its ability to process text rapidly allows for real-time translation of natural language into structured API calls and advanced tool utilization.42 For a financial chatbot, this means the model can instantly understand a prompt such as "How much did I spend on electronics last month?", translate that intent into a specific internal tool request, and formulate an accurate response.43

To support voice-based queries, the architecture employs Amazon Nova Sonic, a state-of-the-art speech-to-speech foundation model.39 Traditional voice assistants rely on fragmented, multi-hop pipelines: Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) converts audio to text, a Large Language Model (LLM) generates a text response, and finally, a Text-to-Speech (TTS) service synthesizes the output audio.44 This multi-hop process introduces significant, noticeable latency that degrades the conversational experience. Nova Sonic circumvents this entirely by processing raw audio streams natively and generating audio responses directly from the neural network.44 This preserves conversational nuance, dramatically reduces latency, and results in a highly fluid, human-like interaction flow.44

### **Context and Session Management via DynamoDB**

A critical functional component of any conversational artificial intelligence is its ability to maintain context across multiple turns of dialogue.40 Foundation models, including the Nova family, are inherently stateless; they do not natively remember prior inputs or system responses unless the entire conversation history is explicitly resubmitted alongside each new user prompt.48

To manage this conversational history efficiently within the strict serverless constraints, the architecture utilizes Amazon DynamoDB as a high-speed, ephemeral memory layer.50 When a user initiates a new chat session from the mobile application, the backend generates a unique cryptographic session\_id. As the user and the assistant exchange messages, a lightweight AWS Lambda function intercepts these interactions and appends them to a dedicated DynamoDB table designed specifically for session state management.50

The DynamoDB schema for session state management is defined with the following structure:

* **Partition Key**: session\_id (String \- uniquely identifying the conversation thread).  
* **Sort Key**: updated\_at (String \- ISO 8601 Timestamp, ensuring chronological ordering).  
* **Attributes**:  
  * messages: A JSON-serialized array containing the entire conversation history, including user prompts and model responses.50  
  * user\_id: A secondary identifier used to enforce strict data isolation between different users' chat logs, preventing cross-contamination of financial data.  
  * ttl: A Time-to-Live numeric attribute defining the exact Unix epoch timestamp when the record should expire.50

The ttl (Time-to-Live) attribute is paramount for automated cost control. It is configured programmatically within the Lambda function to automatically expire and delete session records after exactly 24 hours (e.g., calculated via int(time.time()) \+ 86400).50 This native DynamoDB feature prevents the database storage from swelling indefinitely with obsolete chat logs, thereby minimizing long-term storage costs without requiring custom cleanup scripts, while perfectly maintaining the context window for the duration of any typical user session.50 Upon receiving a new message, the Lambda function executes a GetItem request, retrieves the historical message array, appends the new prompt, utilizes a sliding window algorithm to limit the array length to respect the Nova model's maximum context window, and forwards the heavily contextualized payload to the Amazon Bedrock API.50

### **Multi-Agent Orchestration and WebSocket Integration**

To handle the diverse range of queries a user might submit—ranging from simple factual questions about a specific receipt to complex requests requiring the invocation of analytical tools—the chatbot operates on a multi-agent orchestration pattern utilizing the Amazon Bedrock Converse API.40 This architecture utilizes a graph-based conversation flow to manage state transitions and logic routing.40

When a user submits a query, a primary Supervisor Agent receives the input.45 The Supervisor Agent evaluates the semantic intent of the query. If the user asks a general question, the Supervisor routes the query to a conversational sub-agent. If the query requires hard analytical data (e.g., "Summarize my dining out expenses"), the Supervisor delegates the task to a specialized analytical sub-agent equipped with specific tool configurations.39 The analytical agent utilizes Bedrock's tool calling feature to pause its generation, execute an API request that queries the Timestream or DynamoDB databases, retrieves the precise numerical values, and passes the structured JSON data back into the Nova model.39 The model then synthesizes this raw numerical data into a natural, conversational text response, which is routed back to the user.39

For voice queries originating from the Flutter mobile application, the architecture requires a persistent connection rather than standard HTTP requests.45 The mobile frontend captures audio using the device microphone and establishes a secure WebSocket connection to an AWS API Gateway configured for WebSocket traffic.45 The AWS backend, driven by Lambda, streams these binary audio payloads directly into the Amazon Nova Sonic model via the Bedrock InvokeModelWithBidirectionalStream API.44 This bidirectional streaming capability allows the foundation model to begin processing the audio and formulating a response while the user is still actively speaking, significantly reducing perceived latency.44 The resulting synthesized audio stream is sent back over the WebSocket connection and immediately played through the device speaker, providing a highly responsive, walkie-talkie-style interaction that feels deeply integrated into the application experience.44

## **RAG Architecture and Retrieval Pipeline Patterns**

While the Timestream analytical database flawlessly handles structured numerical data and temporal aggregations, users invariably require the ability to query the unstructured information inherent in their financial documents. This includes the specific itemized text contents of their scanned receipts, complex return policies printed on the back of invoices, or nuanced warranty details. To fulfill this critical requirement, the architecture implements a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) pattern, enabling the chatbot to securely access, search, and precisely cite specific textual documents to ground its responses in factual reality.33

### **Analysis of the RAGStack-Lambda Scale-to-Zero Pattern**

The design of the retrieval pipeline for this application relies heavily on the architectural patterns established in the open-source RAGStack-Lambda repository.14 Traditional enterprise RAG implementations incur substantial, unavoidable standing costs due to the requirement for always-on vector databases and dedicated embedding inference instances. This legacy approach severely violates the strict financial constraints of this serverless project, where idle costs must be eliminated.14 RAGStack-Lambda demonstrates a proven, fully serverless, scale-to-zero architecture that entirely bypasses idle vector database fees.14

The RAGStack-Lambda architecture logically divides the complex RAG process into two distinct, event-driven pipelines orchestrated by AWS Step Functions and AWS Lambda 33:

1. **The Ingestion (Embedding) Pipeline**: When a new document (such as a processed receipt PDF) is uploaded to a designated Amazon S3 bucket, an event triggers a Step Functions state machine.33 A Lambda function within the machine routes the file based on its MIME type.33 Textual data and images are processed using Amazon Nova's highly capable multimodal embeddings.33 This process converts the semantic meaning and visual layout of the document into dense, high-dimensional mathematical vector representations.33 These vectors, alongside extensively extracted metadata (such as extraction confidence scores and timestamps), are then loaded into the backend vector database.33  
2. **The Retrieval Pipeline**: The system exposes a serverless GraphQL API endpoint to handle incoming queries.33 When a user asks a question requiring document retrieval, the GraphQL API invokes a Lambda function that first converts the user's natural language question into a corresponding vector using the identical Amazon Nova embedding model.33 It then executes a similarity search against the vector database to locate mathematically proximal documents, retrieves the highly relevant text chunks, and injects this context directly into the prompt for the Nova 2 Lite LLM.33 The LLM formulates a precise answer, and the API returns the response along with explicit source attribution and collapsible citations linking back to the original receipt image.14

By utilizing serverless compute functions for orchestrating both the ingestion and retrieval pipelines, the system only incurs AWS execution charges during active document processing or active user querying, maintaining a baseline infrastructure cost that rests at zero during idle periods.14

### **Vector Database Selection and Cost Optimization**

The most critical, financially impactful decision in designing a RAG architecture is the selection of the underlying vector database. Amazon Bedrock Knowledge Bases provides a highly attractive, fully managed RAG workflow, automating the complex extraction, chunking, and embedding processes behind a simplified API.56 However, Bedrock Knowledge Bases is not a database itself; it requires a supported, customer-provisioned backend vector store to physically hold and search the embeddings.58

A common default choice for enterprise AWS deployments is Amazon OpenSearch Serverless; however, a rigorous FinOps analysis of its billing structure reveals a significant misalignment with the project's strict cost constraints.16 OpenSearch Serverless charges based on OpenSearch Capacity Units (OCUs). A minimum, highly available deployment requires allocating 2 OCUs continuously, resulting in an unavoidable base infrastructure cost of approximately $345.60 per month, regardless of whether a single query is executed.16 This base cost completely nullifies the financial benefits of the scale-to-zero compute layer and is prohibitive for a consumer application attempting to minimize baseline operational expenditure.16

To circumvent this financial roadblock, the architecture strategically adopts Amazon Aurora PostgreSQL Serverless v2 integrated natively with the open-source pgvector extension.16 Aurora Serverless v2 scales compute capacity down to extremely low Aurora Capacity Units (ACUs) when the database is idle, charging as little as $0.06 per ACU-hour.16 For a small-to-medium knowledge base encompassing localized user receipt data (typically under 100,000 distinct documents), leveraging pgvector on Aurora Serverless v2 reduces vector storage infrastructure costs by up to 87% compared to OpenSearch Serverless.16 This yields an estimated monthly cost closer to $50–$80, while fully supporting the native API integrations required by Amazon Bedrock Knowledge Bases.16 Furthermore, Aurora offers robust, enterprise-grade transactional guarantees and up to three times the throughput of standard open-source PostgreSQL databases, ensuring that rapid vector indexing operations do not bottleneck the upstream receipt ingestion pipeline during periods of high throughput.59

### **Metadata Filtering and Contextual Accuracy**

A fundamental, pervasive challenge in RAG systems is the phenomenon of semantic overlap, where a vector search returns documents that are mathematically similar to the query but entirely incorrect in their contextual application.61 For instance, if a user queries the chatbot, "How much did I spend at the grocery store last week?", a pure, unfiltered vector similarity search might easily retrieve grocery store receipts from two years ago, simply because the semantic content (items purchased, store name) is mathematically identical to the user's current query.61

To definitively solve this hallucination problem, the architecture relies heavily on Metadata Filtering, an advanced capability natively supported by Amazon Bedrock Knowledge Bases.61 During the ingestion pipeline, structured metadata explicitly extracted from the receipt—such as the exact merchant\_name, the verified transaction\_date, and the categorical assignment—are appended as discrete key-value attributes to the generated vector payload.33

When the chatbot receives a natural language query, the Nova 2 Lite model is instructed via rigorous system prompt engineering to extract temporal bounds and categorical constraints from the user's text.43 The multi-agent orchestration layer then applies these extracted constraints as hard filters prior to executing the vector similarity search.61 For example, the user's query translates into a strict database filter specifying transaction\_date \>=. The vector database evaluates this filter first, drastically reducing the search space to only include documents matching the chronological criteria, guaranteeing that the retrieved context is both semantically relevant and chronologically accurate.61 This hybrid search approach—combining rigid metadata filtering with fuzzy semantic vector matching—significantly improves retrieval accuracy, grounds the model in factual data, and virtually eliminates chronological hallucinations in the chatbot's financial summaries.61

## **Synthesis and Strategic Alignment**

The architectural strategy outlined in this document successfully reconciles the highly ambitious business objectives of a modern, data-driven shopping receipts platform with the strict, unyielding operational realities of serverless cloud computing. The architecture is explicitly designed to maximize developer velocity, minimize idle infrastructure costs, and provide a highly responsive, personalized experience to the end-user.

By pioneering a sophisticated design-to-code pipeline anchored by Google Stitch and the DESIGN.md specification, the platform guarantees unprecedented agility in frontend iteration. This approach allows abstract UI adjustments to flow natively into the cross-platform Flutter codebase via autonomous MCP coding agents, entirely bypassing the friction of manual translation and significantly reducing frontend engineering overhead.20

To deliver instantaneous financial insights via the mobile application, the data tier employs a rigorous separation of operational concerns. Amazon Timestream absorbs the immense burden of raw time-series data storage and executes complex, deep analytical aggregations across temporal dimensions.9 Concurrently, Amazon DynamoDB serves as an ultra-fast, precomputed caching layer, delivering dashboard metrics and anonymized peer-group comparisons (calculated via Amazon Athena) directly to the user interface in single-digit milliseconds.4 This hybrid approach perfectly balances analytical depth with UI responsiveness while strictly adhering to the FinOps principles of serverless cost optimization.

Finally, the conversational AI layer redefines user interaction by heavily leveraging the Amazon Nova family of foundation models. Nova 2 Lite provides highly cost-effective, advanced reasoning for text interpretation and complex tool calling, while Nova Sonic enables fluid, real-time voice interactions by eliminating intermediate ASR/TTS translation steps via bidirectional WebSockets.42 The RAG implementation further secures the system by circumventing the prohibitive costs typically associated with standard vector databases. By adopting the scale-to-zero principles modeled in the RAGStack-Lambda repository, and utilizing Aurora Serverless v2 with pgvector alongside Bedrock Knowledge Bases and rigorous metadata filtering, the system ensures that the chatbot remains both financially viable and rigorously accurate in its financial summations.14

This comprehensive synthesis of decoupled analytics, advanced multi-agent AI orchestration, and automated frontend generation establishes a highly resilient, cost-optimized, and deeply extensible foundation. The architecture is fully capable of scaling dynamically with volatile user demand while maintaining the stringent performance and budgetary constraints mandated by the Phase 1 Research and Discovery workflow.
